How to Make a Demo

A practical guide to a strong 3-minute pitch, what judges look for, and how to avoid demo disasters.

A simple 3-minute pitch structure

0:00–0:20

Hook + problem

Say who it’s for and why it matters. One sentence. No history lesson.

0:20–0:50

Your solution

Explain the “before → after” in plain language. If you can’t, simplify.

0:50–2:20

Live demo (the star)

Show the core workflow end-to-end. Narrate what changed for the user.

2:20–2:50

How it works + why it’s hard

One technical detail and one product decision. Keep it crisp.

2:50–3:00

Close

Impact + next step. Example: “Next we’ll add X for Y users.”

What judges usually look for

  • Clarity: Can we understand it fast?
  • Impact: Who benefits and how much?
  • Execution: Does the demo work?
  • Originality: Is the angle unique?
  • Teamwork: Do you know your roles?

Pro move

Start the demo with a “bad” example, then show how your app fixes it.

Slide tips (if you use slides)

Keep it light

3–5 slides max. The product is the presentation.

Big text, one idea

No paragraphs. Use visuals and short phrases.

Demo plan on a slide

A 3-step checklist keeps you on track under pressure.

Live demo vs. video demo

Live demos feel real and confident — but videos are safer if your app depends on flaky internet or complicated setup.

Live demo

  • Feels authentic
  • Shows confidence
  • Risk: bugs happen

Video demo

  • Reliable and smooth
  • Easy to rehearse
  • Risk: feels less “live”

Hybrid strategy

Do a short live demo, but keep a backup video ready in case something breaks.

Avoid demo disasters

  • Rehearse: Twice, with a timer.
  • Use fake data: Never rely on last-minute inputs.
  • One device plan: Test on the exact laptop you’ll present with.
  • Offline backup: Screenshots/video + local copy.
  • Say it calmly: If a bug happens, narrate the intended behavior and move on.
